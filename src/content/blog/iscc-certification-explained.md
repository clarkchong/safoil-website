If you buy waste-based feedstock, you have seen "ISCC-certified" or "ISCC-aligned" on countless offers. But what does the certification actually prove, and what should you look for when it appears on a supplier's documentation? Here is a buyer's-eye view.

## What ISCC is

ISCC — the International Sustainability and Carbon Certification scheme — is one of the main voluntary schemes recognised under the EU Renewable Energy Directive. In practice, it is the framework that lets a tonne of feedstock carry a credible claim about **what it is** and **where it came from**, all the way down the chain to the finished fuel.

For waste and residue feedstocks like UCO, POME or SBEO, that origin claim is the whole point: the material's value depends on it being genuinely waste-derived, and ISCC is how that gets documented in a way regulators accept.

## Chain of custody is the core idea

The concept that does the real work is **chain of custody** — an unbroken documentary trail from collection point to export. Each party in the chain records what they received, what they did to it, and what they passed on.

Most feedstock trading uses a **mass-balance** approach: sustainable and conventional material can be physically mixed, but the sustainable *quantity* is tracked on paper so that only as much is sold as sustainable as actually entered the system. It is an accounting method, not a physical segregation — and understanding that distinction saves buyers a lot of confusion.

## What to actually check

When a supplier presents ISCC documentation, a diligent buyer looks for:

- **A valid certificate** covering the relevant material category and still within its validity period.
- **Consistency between the certificate and the sustainability declaration** that accompanies each shipment — the declaration is the per-consignment document that carries the specific claim.
- **A coherent chain** — the counterparties named should make sense for the material's stated origin.

## "Certified" vs "aligned" — a fair distinction

You will see suppliers describe material as ISCC *certified* versus ISCC-*aligned*. The honest reading: certified means an audited certificate is in hand for that scope; aligned means the operation follows ISCC principles and documentation practices. A good supplier is transparent about which applies to a given stream, rather than blurring the two.
