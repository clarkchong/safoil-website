Every tonne of Used Cooking Oil that leaves a Malaysian port for a European or North American refinery has travelled a longer, more documented road than most buyers see. Here is what actually happens between the fryer and the flexitank.

## 1. Sourcing

It starts with collection — from restaurants, hotels, food manufacturers and the collectors who aggregate from them. This is the least glamorous and most important stage: the integrity of everything downstream depends on knowing, and being able to document, where the oil genuinely came from.

A vetted supplier network is what separates a credible feedstock trade from an opaque one. Collectors who keep proper records, and who can be audited, are worth more than the marginal cents saved buying from those who can't.

## 2. Quality-check and processing

Raw collected oil is variable. Before it can be contracted to a spec, it is filtered to remove solids and food debris, and tested for the parameters that matter to a refiner — free fatty acid, moisture, impurities and unsaponifiables.

Where the material needs to hit a tighter grade, light processing brings it within target. The goal is simple but demanding: **the spec on the contract should equal the spec in the tank**, and not just on the first shipment.

## 3. Verification

This is where traceability becomes tangible. Each batch is sampled and sent for independent laboratory analysis, producing a certificate of analysis that travels with the cargo. Alongside it, the chain-of-custody and sustainability documentation is assembled so the volume can be claimed against the buyer's renewable-energy obligations.

For waste-based feedstock, this paperwork isn't administrative overhead — it is a large part of what the buyer is paying for.

## 4. Export

Finally the material is loaded — typically into flexitanks of around 24 tonnes or into ISO tanks — and shipped FOB from a Malaysian port or CIF to the buyer's discharge port. A trade and logistics desk manages the documentation, booking and shipping so the cargo arrives discharge-ready with a complete file.

## Why the chain matters

Any weak link — an undocumented collector, a skipped test, a missing declaration — and the material either can't be claimed or can't be trusted. The whole point of a disciplined chain is that the buyer receives not just oil, but oil they can **report with confidence**.
