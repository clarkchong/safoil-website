The most interesting corner of the feedstock market right now is the advanced end — the residue streams that qualify under Annex IX of the EU Renewable Energy Directive. POME oil, EFB oil and SBEO sit here, and demand for them tells you a lot about where the renewable-fuels industry is going.

## Why "advanced" is worth a premium

Annex IX feedstocks are genuine waste or residue streams, and under RED II many of them are **double-counting eligible** — meaning each tonne can contribute more toward a renewable-energy obligation than a conventional feedstock. For a producer of renewable diesel or sustainable aviation fuel, that improves both the carbon-intensity profile of the finished fuel and the economics of the deal.

That's the whole reason these materials command a premium. It isn't about the oil being physically superior — it's about what its documented classification unlocks.

## The three streams we watch

**POME oil** — Palm Oil Mill Effluent oil — is recovered from the effluent of palm milling rather than lost to wastewater treatment. As a true residue, it qualifies as an Annex IX advanced feedstock and is double-counting eligible. It is the most established of the three in terms of traded volume.

**EFB oil** — recovered from empty fruit bunch biomass — is more specialised, with tighter availability, and is typically quoted on enquiry against firm demand.

**SBEO** — Spent Bleaching Earth Oil, recovered from the spent clay used in edible-oil refining — is an Annex IX Part A advanced residue, with FFA typically up to around 25%, suited to esterification and HVO routes.

## The outlook: demand outpacing easy supply

As SAF mandates phase in and RED III raises the bar, competition for advanced residues is intensifying. These are byproduct streams — their supply is tied to palm milling and refining throughput, so it can't simply scale to meet appetite. That structural tightness is why we expect the advanced-feedstock premium to persist.

The catch for buyers: **classification is the product.** Without documented chain of custody, an advanced feedstock cannot be claimed as advanced — so the paperwork isn't overhead, it's the value. This is the part of the market where documentation discipline separates a real supplier from a broker with a spreadsheet.
