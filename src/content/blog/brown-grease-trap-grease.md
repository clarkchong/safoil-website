Not all used cooking oil looks the same by the time it reaches a refiner, and brown grease sits at the heavy end of that spectrum — the grade buyers ask about once they've exhausted standard UCO supply.

## What makes it "heavy"

Brown and trap grease are recovered from grease traps and interceptors rather than collected directly from fryers, which means they carry more water, solids and free fatty acids than filtered UCO. FFA content is typically much higher, and the material is thicker and darker — hence the name. It is still a genuine waste-derived feedstock, just one that demands more processing before it is refinery-ready.

## Why buyers still want it

As standard UCO tightens, brown grease offers additional waste-based volume for buyers willing to handle a heavier spec, often at a discount that reflects the extra pre-treatment it requires. For esterification-capable plants, that trade-off can pencil out well.

## Handling considerations

Expect to budget for de-watering and solids removal before the material meets a usable spec, and for heated storage and transport given how readily it thickens below ambient temperature. A certificate of analysis matters even more here than with standard UCO, since FFA and water content can vary widely between loads.
