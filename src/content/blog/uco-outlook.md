Of all the feedstocks we trade, Used Cooking Oil draws the most questions — so it's a fitting place to start a series of product outlooks. Here is how we see the UCO market, and why we expect it to stay firm.

## Demand is structural, not a spike

UCO's rise isn't a passing rally. It is built on regulation that keeps ratcheting in one direction. The EU's Renewable Energy Directive continues to favour waste-based and advanced feedstocks over crop-based oils, and sustainable aviation fuel mandates are adding a whole new class of buyer competing for the same waste lipids. In North America, LCFS and RFS incentives keep renewable diesel margins healthy enough to pull feedstock across the Pacific.

When demand is written into law rather than driven by sentiment, it doesn't evaporate when prices wobble. That's the defining feature of the UCO market right now.

## Supply simply can't sprint

Here is the constraint that shapes everything: you cannot farm used cooking oil. Its supply is capped by how much oil the world cooks with and discards, and that grows slowly. Collection networks can be formalised and tightened — and a lot of value now sits in doing exactly that — but the underlying pool expands at a gentle pace.

Fast-growing, regulation-backed demand meeting slow-growing physical supply is a recipe for firm, and at times volatile, pricing. We don't see that changing.

## What it means for buyers

For a refiner or trader, three practical takeaways follow.

**Traceability is priced in.** Documented, auditable material commands a premium because it can actually be claimed against a mandate. Undocumented volume is worth less regardless of quality.

**Relationships beat spot scrambling.** When supply tightens, buyers with standing offtake arrangements get served first. Spot-only buyers absorb the swings.

**Origin diversification matters.** Concentrating every tonne in one collector or one country is a risk when the whole market is chasing the same supply.
