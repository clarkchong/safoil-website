Palm Fatty Acid Distillate — PFAD — is one of the most widely traded palm co-products, and one of the most misunderstood. This outlook covers what it is, who buys it, and where its market is heading.

## A co-product with two markets

PFAD is what comes off when crude palm oil is physically refined: the free fatty acids stripped out during deodorisation, collected as a high-FFA distillate. Because it is a residue stream rather than a food-grade oil, it has long been a workhorse feedstock — and crucially, it serves **two demand centres at once**.

On one side sits the **oleochemical industry**, turning PFAD into soaps, fatty acids, stearin and countless downstream derivatives. On the other sits **biodiesel and renewable diesel**, where its high free-fatty-acid content suits esterification routes and pre-treatment-capable HVO plants. Two buyers competing for the same barrels tends to put a floor under demand.

## What's shaping the outlook

Malaysia's position as a palm-refining hub makes it one of the most reliable origins for PFAD, and that steady availability is a big part of the appeal. But the outlook is nuanced.

PFAD's sustainability classification differs from waste streams like UCO or POME — a distinction that matters as buyers weigh it against advanced feedstocks for regulated fuel markets. We are transparent about that status so compliance teams can classify it correctly rather than discovering a surprise at audit. For oleochemical buyers, that classification question matters less, and consistent quality and supply take priority.

Expect PFAD to remain a core, liquid feedstock — valued for availability and versatility even as the premium end of the market chases advanced residues.

## Practical notes for buyers

A couple of things worth planning for:

- **PFAD solidifies at ambient temperature**, so logistics mean heated ISO tanks or bulk vessels to ensure a discharge-ready cargo.
- **Grade consistency varies by source**, which is why per-batch testing and a certificate of analysis matter — you want to know the FFA and fatty-matter numbers before the cargo sails.
