EFB oil is one of the smaller-volume advanced feedstocks we trade, but it draws consistent interest from buyers who have exhausted easier sources of Annex IX material.

## Where it comes from

Empty fruit bunches are the fibrous stalks left over once palm fruit has been stripped for milling — for years treated purely as mill biomass or composted on-site. EFB oil is the residual oil recovered from that biomass rather than a separately pressed product, which is precisely why it qualifies as a genuine waste-derived, advanced feedstock rather than a conventional vegetable oil.

## Why it's tightly available

Recovery of oil from empty fruit bunches is more specialised than pressing POME or filtering UCO, and it depends on which mills have invested in the recovery process. That keeps traded volumes thin relative to demand, and most parcels move against firm buyer commitments rather than sitting on a spot list.

## What advanced classification unlocks

As with POME and SBEO, EFB oil's value lies substantially in its documented classification — Annex IX status and, where applicable, double-counting eligibility under RED II. Without a clean chain-of-custody record from mill to export, that classification can't be claimed, so the paperwork is inseparable from the product itself.
