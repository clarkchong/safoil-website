Used Cooking Oil has quietly become one of the most contested feedstocks in the renewable-fuels market. What was once a low-value waste stream is now priced against global biodiesel, renewable diesel and sustainable aviation fuel demand — and the direction of travel into 2026 is worth understanding for any buyer building a feedstock programme.

## The demand side is tightening

Three regulatory forces are pulling on the same pool of waste oil at once.

**EU RED III** raises the ceiling on renewable-energy targets in transport and continues to favour waste-based and advanced feedstocks over crop-based oils. That structurally advantages UCO, POME and similar streams.

**SAF mandates** are the newer pressure. As sustainable aviation fuel blending obligations phase in across Europe and elsewhere, refiners are competing for the same waste lipids that biodiesel producers already rely on. Aviation's willingness to pay a premium ripples back through the whole feedstock chain.

**North American incentives** — LCFS in California and the federal RFS — keep renewable diesel margins attractive enough to pull feedstock across the Pacific, adding a second major demand centre to what used to be a largely European story.

## The supply side can't simply scale

The awkward truth about UCO is that supply is capped by how much cooking oil the world uses and discards. You cannot farm more of it. Collection networks can be tightened and formalised — and that is where a lot of value now sits — but the underlying volume grows slowly.

That mismatch between fast-growing, regulation-driven demand and slow-growing physical supply is the core reason prices have firmed and stayed volatile.

## What this means for buyers

For a refiner or trader, three things follow:

- **Traceability is now priced in.** Material with clean chain-of-custody documentation commands a premium because it can actually be claimed against a mandate. Undocumented volume is worth less, regardless of its quality.
- **Long-term relationships beat spot scrambling.** When supply is tight, buyers with standing offtake arrangements get served first. Spot-only buyers absorb the volatility.
- **Origin diversification matters.** Concentrating all volume in one country or one collector is a risk when everyone is chasing the same tonnes.
