Tallow rendered from animal fat has quietly become one of the steadier feedstocks in the renewable-fuels supply chain — less discussed than used cooking oil, but increasingly important as refiners diversify away from a single waste stream.

## A by-product with built-in supply

Unlike UCO, which depends on how much oil the world cooks with, tallow supply tracks the meat and rendering industry — a large, steady, non-seasonal source. That gives it a different risk profile: less price drama tied to collection-network tightness, more tied to livestock and rendering throughput. For a feedstock book that leans heavily on UCO or palm residues, tallow is often the diversification play.

## Grade and classification matter

Tallow is not one product. Rendered animal fat is typically separated into categories based on its source material, and that classification determines whether a given parcel is eligible feedstock for biodiesel and renewable diesel under regulations like RED II/III, or destined for oleochemical and feed-grade use instead. A buyer's first question should always be which category and grade is on offer, backed by documentation — not just an FFA number.

## Practical notes for buyers

Tallow solidifies well above ambient temperature in many climates, so cargoes typically move in heated ISO tanks or as a bulk vessel parcel, similar to PFAD. Per-batch testing for FFA, moisture and impurities, alongside a certificate of analysis, is essential — rendering quality varies more by source than most buyers expect.
